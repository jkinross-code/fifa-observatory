FIFA World Cup Commercial Social Health Observatory

This observatory tracks the commercial health impact of FIFA World Cup sponsorship across six tournaments (2006–2026). It quantifies exposure to harmful marketing — HFSS food and drinks, alcohol, fossil fuels, and gambling — and measures the health vulnerability of participating nations using the Participating Nations Health Impact Score (PNHIS).

Data sources: WHO Global Health Observatory, World Bank, FIFA commercial disclosures, Alfayad et al. 2022 (broadcast impression data), regulatory assessments. Evidence tiers: A = confirmed data; D = modelled estimates.

What is the PNHIS Score?

The Participating Nations Health Impact Score (PNHIS) is a composite index measuring the burden of commercial health harm experienced by populations whose national team participates in the FIFA World Cup. It captures three negative dimensions and one protective dimension:

BHVI — Background Health Vulnerability Index (weight: 35%)
Baseline population health risk from obesity, alcohol consumption, physical inactivity, and NCD mortality. Higher BHVI = more vulnerable population.
CED — Commercial Exposure Dose (weight: 40%)
Estimated total unhealthy marketing impressions delivered to the population, adjusted for regulatory strength. Higher regulatory protection = lower CED.
RGS — Regulatory Gap Score (weight: 25%)
Weakness of the nation's advertising regulations (HFSS food, alcohol, gambling). Low regulatory protection = high RGS harm score.
PHBP — Public Health Benefit Potential (positive offset)
Protective factor. Credit for strong health infrastructure (high life expectancy, low inactivity, strong GDP). Partially offsets the harm score.
Formula: PNHIS = −[(BHVI × 0.35) + (CED × 0.40) + (RGS × 0.25)] + PHBP
Range approximately −100 (extreme harm) to +25 (protective). Negative scores indicate net commercial health harm. Data sources: WHO Global Health Observatory, World Bank, regulatory assessments.

The Commercial Social Health (CSH) Score quantifies the net population health impact of a company's marketing and sponsorship activities in the context of FIFA World Cup. It integrates product harm, marketing reach, audience composition, regulatory context, and documented health outcome associations.
CSH Score Methodology
Formula
CSH = −[(PHW × RI × AC × RG) + (NR × 0.3)] + [(PE × 0.2) + (ETH × 0.15)]
Negative CSH = net harm. Positive CSH = net benefit. Range: −100 to +100.
PHW
Product Harm Weight
Intrinsic harm of the product category. Alcohol=1.0, HFSS food=0.8, sugary drinks=0.7, fossil fuel=0.6, gambling=0.9, tobacco=1.2, neutral=0
RI
Reach Index
Normalised marketing reach (impressions per 100M population). Derived from UMI table and broadcast/social impressions.
AC
Audience Composition
Multiplier for child audience proportion. Range 1.0–2.0. If >20% impressions reach under-16s, multiplier = 1.5×. If >40%, 2.0×.
RG
Regulatory Gap
Fraction of exposure occurring outside regulatory protection. Sponsorship exposure (no CAP/BCAP jurisdiction) scores 1.0. Post-watershed TV scores 0.3.
NR
Normalised Revenue
Estimated sponsorship fee as fraction of max (USD 150M). Proxy for commercial influence over the event's regulatory environment.
PE
Positive Externalities
Credit for documented health-positive activities (health campaigns, sustainability initiatives, charity commitments). Max 20 points.
ETH
Ethics Score
Normalised ESG/B Corp/Ethisphere score (0–100 → 0–15 point contribution). Rewards good governance, labour, supply chain.

2026 (USA/CAN/MEX)

Harm channel: in-app immersive retention + live odds + pitch-side branding
The Stats Perform mechanism (NBC New York, 2026)
Under a 4-year deal through 2029, Stats Perform (parent of Opta) grants licensed gambling operators the right to livestream World Cup matches directly inside betting apps. Opta supplies real-time official stats, live scores and match trackers to gambling clients simultaneously. This is not a commercial sponsorship — it is an infrastructure deal that embeds FIFA's product within the betting consumption interface. It bypasses traditional advertising regulation entirely: it is not a "gambling ad", it is a "sports stream" that happens to live inside a betting platform.
US context amplifier: 38 states with legal sports betting
The 2026 tournament is hosted in the US — the world's fastest-growing sports betting market post-PASPA (2018). US-licensed operators (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM) hold the right to stream World Cup content to their combined account bases of ~40 million registered users. Problem gambling prevalence in the US is estimated at 2.1% (2026, GBD modelled) and rising. This is the highest-risk gambling exposure context in World Cup history.
Why UMI under-captures this
The UMI score captures logo impressions and broadcast ad exposure. The Stats Perform mechanism operates at the consumption interface layer — it is invisible to impression-based measurement but structurally more potent: it maximises time-on-platform and co-locates match content with in-play betting opportunities.
Direction of travel
2006: state lottery logo. 2022: first betting sponsor + broadcast ads. 2026: betting operators as official content distribution partners. The trajectory is toward deeper integration, not arms-length sponsorship. 2034 (Saudi Arabia) will structurally entrench gambling infrastructure further given the kingdom's Tier 1 sports betting market ambitions.
Sources: NBC New York (Stats Perform/FIFA streaming deal, 2026); ESPN (Betano regional deal); Yahoo Sports (ADI Predictstreet ~$150M); McGrane et al. Addictive Behaviors Reports 2026 (PMC12854864). Stats Perform deal value not publicly disclosed.

Unhealthy Marketing Index (UMI)
UMI = log₁₀(total_impressions + 1) × category_harm_weight × (data_completeness_pct / 100)
Child UMI Extension
Child UMI applies a ×1.5 vulnerability multiplier to the harm weight for under-16 audiences, consistent with CAP/BCAP guidance that recognises children's heightened susceptibility to food and alcohol marketing. The child UMI is anchored to confirmed under-16 impression data from Alfayad et al. 2022 (759M HFSS impressions, 385M alcohol impressions, UK 2018) and scaled to other tournaments using the revenue index × completeness penalty.
Correlated Marketing Exposure Index — Important Caveat
The corporate overlay displayed on the Health Trends chart is a correlated marketing exposure index, not an estimate of attributable fraction. It represents: FIFA commercial revenue index × category harm weight × reach proxy, interpolated across years. It is monotonically non-decreasing by design, reflecting the empirical growth in FIFA revenues, match count, and sponsor count since 2006. Causal inference would require controlling for income growth, concurrent food/alcohol industry trends, policy changes, and other confounders. For causal estimates, see McGrane et al. 2026 (gambling, quasi-experimental) and Alfayad et al. 2022 (broadcast exposure, observational with denominator data).

Commercial Social Health (CSH) Score
CSH = −[(PHW × RI × AC × RG) + (NR × 0.3)] + [(PE × 0.2) + (ETH × 0.15)]

Range: −100 (maximum harm) to +100 (maximum benefit). Negative = net population health harm from marketing activities.

Worked example — AB InBev (2026):
PHW = 1.0 (alcohol) · RI = 0.6 (Tier 2 reach) · AC = 1.5 (child audience multiplier) · RG = 1.0 (sponsorship, no CAP/BCAP)
Harm component = (1.0 × 0.6 × 1.5 × 1.0) + (0.3 × 0.3) = 0.90 + 0.09 = 0.99
Benefit = 0 (no documented health programmes)
CSH = −99.0

Limitations: Reach Index (RI) currently uses tier proxy (Tier 1 = 0.9, Tier 2 = 0.6, Tier 3 = 0.3) pending impression data for all sponsors. Positive externalities (PE) require manual input. Ethics score (ETH) requires population of company_ethics_scores table. CSH scores marked with * are tier-proxy estimates.

Data Quality Standards
A
Tier A — Peer-reviewed
Peer-reviewed journal article with impression counts + child exposure split. Only source: Alfayad et al. 2022 (UK 2018 broadcast).
B
Tier B — Official reports
Regulator / WHO / NGO report with appearance counts but no full impression modelling.
C
Tier C — Trade press
Confirms advertiser presence, no counts. Not used in UMI calculations.
D
Tier D — Inferred
Modelled or inferred. Flagged explicitly. Excluded from primary analysis.
Data status vocabulary: confirmed · estimated · presence_only · not_found · not_searched · restricted · not_applicable
Critical distinction: not_found (searched, zero result) ≠ not_searched (no search conducted) ≠ not_applicable (platform didn't exist)
Sensitivity Analysis — UMI Harm Weights
How UMI scores change under alternative harm weight assumptions. Base case weights derived from GBD 2023 disability weights and Boyland et al. meta-analysis. Ranges reflect plausible alternative framings from the literature.
Interpretation: UMI scores are robust to ±0.2 variation in HFSS and sugary drink weights (base case 0.8 and 0.7). Alcohol scores are most sensitive to weight choice — an upper bound of 2.0 (reflecting cancer-only DALY burden) would increase total UMI by ~40% for alcohol rows. Gambling weight is least contested in the literature (harm is acute and measurable via McGrane 2026). The base-case harm weight hierarchy (alcohol > gambling > HFSS > sugary drinks > fossil fuel) is consistent with WHO Global Status Report 2022 NCD risk factor rankings.
Data Gaps Register
Systematic documentation of known data gaps, access barriers, and primary data collection requirements. Critical for Lancet Public Health peer review transparency.
Primary data collection requirements for full UMI coverage: (1) BARB/Nielsen broadcast impression data for UK 2022 (estimated cost £8,000–£15,000 per tournament year); (2) Meta Ad Library systematic scrape for branded HFSS/alcohol content during 2022 and 2026 match periods (free but requires automated collection); (3) Replication of Alfayad 2022 methodology for US/Canada/Mexico broadcasts using Nielsen North America data; (4) Child exposure split (u16) for all non-UK markets — requires audience survey linkage. Until primary data collection, all non-GB non-2018 UMI scores remain Tier D estimates and should be presented as such in publication.
Methods Appendix — Lancet Public Health Format
Structured methods summary following Lancet Public Health reporting standards. For supplementary materials submission. STROBE checklist items noted where applicable.
Study Design & Setting
Design: Cross-sectional observational study with longitudinal ecological trend analysis. Mixed-methods: sponsor database linkage, broadcast content analysis, and health outcomes correlation.
Setting: FIFA World Cup tournaments 2006–2026 (6 tournaments, 8 host nations). Global scope with primary impression data from United Kingdom (broadcast).
Period: FIFA commercial partnership data 2003–2026. Health outcome data 1990–2023 (IHME GBD 2023, WHO GHO).
STROBE items 4, 5, 6
Data Sources & Variables
Sponsor data: FIFA official partner announcements, FIFA Financial Reports 2007–2024, Inside FIFA press releases. N=100 sponsor entries across 6 tournaments.
Impression data: Alfayad et al. (2022) — UK 2018 broadcast, Tier A (peer-reviewed, impression counts, u16 split). All other impressions: Tier D estimates (see Data Quality Standards).
Health outcomes: IHME GBD 2023 (DALYs, mortality); WHO GHO (obesity, alcohol per capita); NCD-RisC 2024 (child obesity).
STROBE items 7, 8, 9
UMI Construction
Formula: UMI = log₁₀(impressions + 1) × harm_weight × (completeness/100)
Anchor: Alfayad 2022 UK 2018 confirmed impressions (HFSS: 7,500M → UMI 3.08; alcohol: 3,300M → UMI 5.22).
Scaling: Non-anchor rows scaled by FIFA commercial revenue index (2006=0.43 → 2026=1.63), match count ratio, and completeness penalty. Child UMI applies ×1.5 vulnerability multiplier (CAP/BCAP rationale).
Constraint: Monotonically non-decreasing — justified by empirical growth in FIFA revenue, match count, and sponsor count in every cycle.
STROBE items 10, 11
Causal Inference Framework
The correlated marketing exposure index is not an estimate of attributable fraction. True causal inference would require:
  • Counterfactual: consumption trends in comparable non-WC years or non-host markets
  • Confounder control: income growth, food environment, concurrent policy changes, baseline NCD rates
  • Dose-response: exposure quantified at individual (not ecological) level
Strongest causal evidence available: McGrane et al. 2026 (gambling, quasi-experimental ITV vs BBC, IRR 1.16–1.24). All other associations are ecological.
STROBE items 12, 16, 19
Limitations
  • Impression data confirmed for UK 2018 only (Tier A). All other tournaments estimated (Tier D).
  • No broadcast coding for social media impressions 2006–2022 (Meta Ad Library coverage limited pre-2023).
  • FIFA commercial revenue used as reach proxy — actual per-category spend allocation not publicly disclosed.
  • UMI cannot be disaggregated by individual brand vs category without primary impression data.
  • Health outcome data from ecological sources — cannot establish individual-level exposure-outcome relationships.
  • Harm weights are literature-derived point estimates; uncertainty ranges shown in sensitivity analysis.
  • Child exposure (u16) available for UK 2018 only. Child UMI for other years is modelled.
STROBE items 17, 19
Proposed Primary Study Protocol
To achieve Tier A evidence for 2026 US broadcast:
  1. Content analysis of ≥13 Fox/Telemundo 2026 match broadcasts (replicating Alfayad methodology)
  2. Nielsen North America audience data linkage for u18 exposure split
  3. Social media: systematic Meta Ad Library + TikTok Creative Center data collection
  4. Comparison arm: non-WC broadcasts in same time windows (natural control)
Estimated resource: 2 researchers × 8 weeks. NIHR/MRC Public Health Research scheme eligible (commercial determinants theme).
Informed by: Alfayad 2022 methodology; Noel 2017 Alcohol Alcoholism

PNHIS — Participating Nations Health Impact Score

The Participating Nations Health Impact Score (PNHIS) is a composite index ranging from −100 to +25 that estimates the projected population health risk associated with commercial exposure during FIFA World Cup participation. It is not a measured outcome.

PNHIS = −[(BHVI × 0.35) + (CED × 0.40) + (RGS × 0.25)] + PHBP
Component Definitions
BHVI — Baseline Health Vulnerability Index
35%
Normalised composite of WHO/World Bank indicators: obesity (20%), physical inactivity (10%), alcohol per capita (20%), NCD mortality (15%), diabetes (10%), life expectancy inverted (5%), GDP per capita inverted (10%). Normalised min-max across all teams in the tournament year.
CED — Commercial Exposure Dose
40%
matches_played × tournament_UMI_per_match × (1 − mean_RPS). UMI per match: 2006=0.18, 2010=0.22, 2014=0.28, 2018=0.58, 2022=0.65, 2026=0.82. Normalised across all teams per tournament.
RGS — Regulatory Gap Score
25%
1 − mean(HFSS score, alcohol score, gambling score) from national regulatory data. Reflects how well national regulations protect populations from unhealthy commercial exposure.
PHBP — Positive Health Benefit (bonus)

Physical activity benefit = (inactivity_rate / 100) × 10. Host nations receive +5 economic benefit; others +2. Minimum 0, maximum 25.
Score interpretation: <−60 = very high risk (red); −60 to −30 = high risk (orange); −30 to 0 = moderate risk (yellow); >0 = net positive (green).
Data sources: WHO GHO (obesity, alcohol, NCD mortality, inactivity, diabetes, life expectancy), World Bank WDI (GDP per capita), regulatory data from national legislation assessments.
